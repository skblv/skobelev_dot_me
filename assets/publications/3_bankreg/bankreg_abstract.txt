U.S. bank regulators generally view their role as overseeing banks' exposure to climate-related risks rather than actively shaping climate policy. However, the boundary between risk oversight and policy advocacy is a gray area. We examine how U.S. bank regulators navigate this boundary by analyzing the rulemaking process for the 2023 interagency guidance on climate-related financial risk management. Our analysis consists of two parts. First, we examine the role of public pressure in shaping the final guidance. Using large language models (LLMs) to classify the public comments submitted on the draft, we find that the comments are highly polarized: the majority of individuals and climate advocacy groups support climate action by bank regulators, while banks and banking associations oppose it. We then construct textual alignment measures between each comment and the corresponding paragraphs in the draft and final guidance, and find that highly climate-engaged comments are less likely to be reflected in the final version, suggesting regulators discounted pro-climate advocacy. Second, we use LLMs to simulate regulators operating under different incentives. We implement a two-stage procedure in which the LLM first decides which public comments a regulator in a given role would accept, then revises the draft guidance using only those accepted comments. The evidence at the first stage is robust: both commercial and fine-tuned LLMs systematically accept or reject comments in line with their assigned role. In the second stage, we find suggestive evidence that regulators' revisions resemble those of a risk-averse bureaucrat most closely. However, the second stage outcomes are sensitive to the choice of model, echoing cautions in the literature on using LLMs as human surrogates.
